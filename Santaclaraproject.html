Introduction

In Ventura County, water looks ordinary. It comes out of sinks, keeps lawns green, moves through irrigation lines, and fills golf course ponds. But beneath that familiar surface lies a hidden system — aquifers under pressure, recharge basins, imported pipelines, and communities that carry risks most people never see. This is a story about the lower Santa Clara River watershed, and what water reveals about who gets to feel secure, and who doesn't.

02 — The Water We See

Green on the Surface

Lawns, golf courses, parks, and irrigated fields can make a dry region look more water-secure than it really is. Green grass feels normal, healthy, even beautiful — but it hides the labor, pumping, treatment, and management needed to keep it that way.

Where does Oxnard's water come from?

Local Groundwater~25–30%
United Water Conservation District~30–40%
Calleguas Imported Supply~30–40%

"A golf course, a lawn, and a strawberry field can all look green on the surface, while the watershed underneath deals with groundwater stress, seawater intrusion, and unequal environmental risk."

Oxnard's Advanced Water Purification Facility sells recycled water to agricultural users and River Ridge Golf Course. Some green spaces run on recycled water — but the visual message still matters. The question is not whether green space is bad. The question is: where does that water come from, and who bears the cost?

03

The Water We Don't See

Aquifers, recharge basins, pipelines — the invisible infrastructure beneath every green landscape.

03 — The Water We Don't See

The Hidden System

Beneath every visible landscape is an invisible infrastructure — groundwater basins, recharge ponds, irrigation pipelines, and imported water. The Santa Clara River watershed's real story happens underground.

Aquifers & Pumping

Groundwater basins beneath the Oxnard Plain pump roughly 170,000 acre-feet per year — nearly three times what's recharged annually.

Saltwater Intrusion

Over-pumping lowered coastal aquifer levels below sea level, allowing seawater to invade a 16-square-mile zone near Point Mugu.

Recharge Basins

United Water Conservation District diverts surface water into recharge ponds, adding ~63,000 acre-feet annually back into aquifers.

Imported Water

Calleguas Municipal Water District brings water from distant sources, supplying 30–40% of Oxnard's total needs.

Recycled Water

Oxnard's Advanced Water Purification Facility treats wastewater for agricultural and industrial reuse, reducing pressure on potable supplies.

Irrigation Pipelines

Pleasant Valley County Water District uses river diversions to serve Camarillo-area farmland, reducing groundwater dependence.

"The region extracts nearly three times more water than it recharges. Most residents never directly see the system that keeps the surface green."

04

Who Carries the Risk?

Water security in Ventura County is not equally distributed.

04 — Who Carries the Risk?

Uneven Ground

Some residents experience water as reliable and clean. Some landscapes remain green and maintained. But beneath that stability are communities whose risks are easier to overlook — and who rarely appear in the policy conversation.

Farmworkers

Work in heat and drought with direct exposure to water quality issues, irrigation runoff, and agricultural pesticides in groundwater.

Coastal residents

Near saltwater intrusion zones where groundwater is increasingly brackish, threatening drinking water quality along the Oxnard coastline.

Groundwater-dependent communities

With 25–30% of supply from local groundwater, contamination from nitrogen, bacteria, and agricultural discharge creates ongoing risk.

Floodplain neighborhoods

Development in flood-prone areas increases runoff, erosion, and habitat loss — compounding water stress in both wet and dry years.

"Every new surface, building, pipe, and irrigation demand changes the relationship between people and watershed — and not everyone bears that change equally."

05

Water as Gift

Robin Wall Kimmerer and the idea of reciprocity.

05 — Water as Gift

Reciprocity & Responsibility

Robin Wall Kimmerer's "The Gift of Strawberries" offers a different framework. In a market economy, water is a resource — to secure, allocate, consume. In a gift economy, receiving something creates responsibility toward what gave it.

"If water is a gift, then using it creates obligations of care, gratitude, and reciprocity — not just toward the infrastructure that delivers it, but toward the watershed itself."

Applying Kimmerer's lens shifts the central question. Not just: do we have enough? But: what does the watershed give, what do we take, and what forms of return are owed?

  • Protect groundwater recharge areas instead of treating them as empty, developable land
  • Recognize farmworkers and lower-income residents as central — not peripheral — to the water story
  • Reduce water use that exists mainly to maintain appearances of abundance
  • Support recycled water and conservation without pretending technology solves structural inequity
  • Learn the names, histories, and vulnerabilities of local watersheds
