Where Are We?

In Ventura County, water can look ordinary — it comes out of sinks, keeps lawns green, fills golf course ponds. This project focuses on the lower Santa Clara River watershed and the connected Oxnard Plain and Pleasant Valley water system.

03

The Water We Don't See

Beneath every visible landscape is a hidden system — groundwater basins, recharge ponds, irrigation pipelines, imported water, and recycled water infrastructure. The Santa Clara River watershed's story is largely underground.

  • Aquifers & groundwater basins pumped heavily across the Oxnard Plain
  • Saltwater intrusion in a 16-sq-mile coastal zone near Point Mugu
  • Recharge ponds that replenish aquifers with surface water diversions
  • Irrigation pipelines serving farms across Pleasant Valley
  • Recycled water treated and redistributed for non-potable uses
  • Imported water brought in from distant sources through Calleguas
170kacre-feet pumped from Oxnard Plain wells annually
63kacre-feet recharged back into basins each year

The region extracts nearly three times more water than it recharges. These numbers show how much the community depends on water most residents never directly see.

04

Who Carries the Risk?

Water security in Ventura County is uneven. Not everyone experiences water the same way. Stability visible in some neighborhoods often rests on systems that distribute risk — and that risk doesn't fall equally.

Farmworkers

Work in heat and drought conditions. Directly exposed to water quality issues, irrigation runoff, and agricultural pesticides in groundwater.

Coastal residents

Near saltwater intrusion zones. Groundwater increasingly brackish, affecting drinking water quality along the Oxnard coastline.

Groundwater-dependent communities

25–30% of Oxnard's supply is local groundwater. Contamination from nitrogen, bacteria, and agricultural discharge creates ongoing risk.

Floodplain neighborhoods

Development in flood-prone areas increases runoff velocity, erosion risk, and habitat loss — compounding water stress in wet and dry years alike.

"Every new surface, building, pipe, and irrigation demand changes the relationship between people and watershed."

05

Water as Gift

Robin Wall Kimmerer's "The Gift of Strawberries" offers a different way of thinking about water. In a market economy, water is a resource to secure and consume. In a gift economy, receiving something creates responsibility.

"If water is a gift, then using it creates obligations of care, gratitude, and reciprocity."

Kimmerer's lens asks us to think beyond management. Not just: do we have enough? But: what does the watershed give, what do we take, and what forms of return are owed?

  • Protect groundwater recharge areas instead of treating them as empty land
  • Recognize farmworkers and lower-income residents as part of the water story
  • Reduce water use that mainly maintains appearances
  • Support recycled water and conservation without pretending technology solves everything
  • Learn the names, histories, and vulnerabilities of local watersheds

Water scarcity is not only a shortage — it is a relationship. The way a community uses water reveals what it values, what it hides, and who it asks to carry environmental risk.
